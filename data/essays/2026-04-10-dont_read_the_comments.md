# Don't Read the Comments
*April 2026*

Aaron Swartz told us not to read the comments.

He was right. He was almost certainly right. The comment section of the early-to-mid internet was a place where nuance went to die and bad faith went to thrive. "DON'T READ THE COMMENTS" became a survival heuristic for anyone who published anything online. It was self-care before we had that word for it.

I've always had a hard time following that advice.

I read the comments. I've always read the comments. On Hacker News threads about my projects, on Reddit posts about Python packaging, on GitHub issues that were really personal grievances disguised as bug reports. I know you're not supposed to. I know it's better for your mental health to ship the thing and walk away. I read them anyway.

There's something in me that wants to know what people think. Not in an approval-seeking way — or maybe partly in an approval-seeking way, I'm being honest here — but more in a "the conversation is the interesting part" way. The thing you build is a starting point. What people do with it, how they react to it, what it makes them think about — that's where it gets interesting.

## The Clean Quiet

For all that, I've enjoyed not having comments on this site.

This site has been a quiet place. You come here, you read something, you leave. Maybe you send me an email. Maybe you don't. The writing exists on its own terms, without a peanut gallery appended to the bottom of every piece. There's a peace to that. The essay is the essay. It doesn't have a reply section slowly accumulating opinions underneath it like sediment.

I liked that. I still like that.

There's a reason a lot of writers keep their sites comment-free. It changes the energy of the writing when you know there's a response section waiting at the bottom. You start anticipating objections. You start hedging. You start writing for the comment section instead of writing for the thing you actually want to say. Without comments, the page is yours. The silence at the bottom of the essay is a kind of respect — for the reader's private reaction, for the writer's right to say a thing without immediately defending it.

So why am I adding comments?

## The Old Web

Because I've been thinking a lot about the old web lately.

The web I fell in love with had comment sections. It had blogrolls and trackbacks and people responding to each other's posts with their own posts. It was messy and sometimes hostile and often wonderful. It was a web where you could write something and someone across the world could respond, and then you'd respond to their response, and suddenly you were in a conversation with a stranger about something that mattered to both of you.

Social media ate that web. Twitter became the comment section for everything. Then Twitter ate itself. Now we have a fragmented landscape where conversations happen in a dozen places, none of them attached to the thing being discussed. The writing lives here, the reactions live over there, and the connective tissue between them is gone.

I keep [building things](/essays/2026-04-09-building_a_home_for_twenty_thousand_photographs) that try to recapture some of what the old web felt like. A personal site instead of a social media profile. Long-form writing instead of threads. An [actual photography platform](https://photos.kennethreitz.org) instead of an Instagram grid. This is another small step in that direction.

## The Experiment

So I've added [giscus](https://giscus.app/) to the site. It's a comment system backed by GitHub Discussions. You authenticate with your GitHub account, your comment becomes a discussion on the repo, and it renders inline at the bottom of the post. No anonymous drive-bys. No separate account to create. If you have a GitHub account, you can leave a thought.

I chose giscus specifically because of the GitHub connection. The audience for this site skews technical. Most of you already have GitHub accounts. And there's something fitting about the comments living in the same repository as the content — the conversation becomes part of the project, versioned and searchable alongside the writing itself.

Here's what I want to be clear about: this is an experiment, not a commitment.

I'm trying it out. If it adds something — if interesting conversations happen, if people share perspectives I hadn't considered, if it makes the site feel more alive — then the comments stay. If it turns into noise, or changes the energy of the writing in ways I don't like, or just sits there empty (which is also fine, honestly), then I'll pull it out. No drama. The toggle is easy.

I've learned, slowly and sometimes painfully, that you can try things without committing to them forever. You can hold something lightly. You can say "let's see" without it meaning "this is who I am now."

## The Contradiction

There's a contradiction here and I'm aware of it. I'm the person who enjoyed the quiet, adding a megaphone to the bottom of every page. I'm the person who knows better than to read the comments, installing infrastructure specifically designed to generate comments for me to read.

But maybe that's okay. Maybe the interesting life is the one where you hold contradictions instead of resolving them. I want the quiet and I want the conversation. I want the essay to stand on its own and I want to know what you think about it. Both things are true. The comment section at the bottom of this post is me trying to hold both at once.

Aaron Swartz was right. Don't read the comments.

I'm going to read the comments.
