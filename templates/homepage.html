The Algorithm Eats

My most urgent work examines how engagement-optimized algorithms systematically consume the foundations of human flourishing. This isn't abstract critique—these are patterns I've observed while living through the largest psychological experiment in human history. Every metric that matters for human wellbeing has declined as algorithmic optimization has accelerated. These systems don't just change what we see—they transform how we think, what we value, and who we become.

Through personal observation, I trace how optimization for engagement creates recursive loops that consume their own substrates: virtue, language, love, democracy, reality, and time—until finally, in the algorithm eats itself, revealing the first crack in an otherwise totalizing system.

Mental Health & Technology

Beyond the sanitized wellness culture lies the messy technical reality of consciousness maintenance in digital environments. This work emerged from living with schizoaffective disorder in an industry that claims to support mental health awareness while systematically discriminating against people with actual psychiatric conditions. These essays cut through therapeutic platitudes to examine how technology either supports or systematically undermines psychological functioning.

These essays trace a decade-long journey from breaking silence about psychiatric conditions to exposing how technological systems exploit psychological vulnerabilities: MentalHealthError, what mental health actually is, algorithmic psychological damage, AI reality-checking, and the professional cost of honesty about psychological reality.

I believe simple tools should feel natural to use. When I created Requests, the goal was straightforward: make HTTP in Python feel as simple as it should be. The inspiration came from frustration with Python's built-in HTTP libraries. urllib2 required dozens of lines for basic requests. I thought: "There has to be a better way to do this." Requests was the answer—now downloaded over 500 million times monthly.

The best tools don't just work—they feel right. Whether it's an API call or an error message, I try to design things the way developers actually think.

Design Philosophy

I try to build things that make developers' lives easier, not harder.

Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication.

Behind every import requests is someone trying to solve a problem or get something done. If the tool is confusing or awkward, that's friction they don't need. This empathy comes from years of being the frustrated developer myself—dealing with XML configuration files, unclear error messages, and APIs that force you to think like the machine instead of expressing your intent naturally.

When people use your code daily, you want it to feel helpful rather than frustrating.
